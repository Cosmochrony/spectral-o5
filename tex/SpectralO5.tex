  \begin{abstract}
    The Cosmochrony relaxation cascade generates an inter-generational mass hierarchy through
    the contraction of the Kesten--McKay spectral support as the effective valence $p(n)$ grows.
    The cascade exponent $\beta$ governing $p(n) \sim n^\beta$ was identified in companion papers
    as the sole remaining structural parameter of the admissibility sub-programme: O3 constrains
    $\betastar \in (0.09, 0.13)$ from lepton masses, while O4 derives the quadratic upper bound
    $p(n) \lesssim Cn^2$ from bounded-flux and Cheeger arguments, leaving a factor of
    $10$--$20$ unexplained.

    The present paper addresses this gap by introducing the notion of \emph{admissible frontier}:
    the subset of the raw BFS boundary whose elements introduce genuinely new directions in the
    admissible spectral subspace.
    We prove that the vertex-based admissible frontier saturates completely at scale
    $O(|\Cl(G)|) = O(q)$, far below $|G| = O(q^3)$, through a direct consequence of the
    representation theory of $G = \PSL(2, \F_q)$.
    This Proposition is a theorem, independent of any phenomenological input.

    We then examine transition-based refinements.
    Character-based transition fingerprints collapse to the same saturation behaviour on LPS
    graphs, where all generators lie in a single conjugacy class.
    Fixed-dimensional matrix proxies produce a clear pre-saturation decay of the admissible
    frontier fraction but with a $q$-independent saturation threshold, disqualifying them as
    structural mechanisms.
    A Steinberg-based fingerprint using the permutation action on $\mathbb{P}^1(\F_q)$
    achieves the first $q$-structural saturation ($|S^*| \sim q^{1.15}$, $|S^*|/|G| \to 0$),
    but the pre-saturation window remains too short to extract a reliable effective exponent.

    We conclude that the smallness of $\betastar$ is not a representation-theoretic dimension
    effect but a genuinely dynamical redundancy phenomenon, and identify $\dim \mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{mat}}
    = O(q^2)$ as the minimal ambient dimension for a structurally complete theory.
    The correct class of mechanisms is isolated; quantitative extraction of the exponent is
    deferred to the next paper.
  \end{abstract}

  \textbf{Keywords:} admissible frontier; cascade exponent; $\PSL(2,\F_q)$;
  LPS Ramanujan graphs; Kesten--McKay measure; representation theory; Steinberg representation;
  mass hierarchy; spectral admissibility.

  \section{Introduction}
    \label{sec:introduction}

    \subsection{Background and motivation}

      The Cosmochrony framework~\cite{Cosmochrony} posits that physical observables arise through
      a non-injective projection $\Pi\colon \Omega_\chi \to \mathcal{O}$, where $\Omega_\chi$ is a
      pre-geometric relational substrate.
      The non-injectivity is not a defect but a structural feature responsible for bounded projective
      capacity, emergent gauge structure, and finite resolution~\cite{Beau2026c}.

      Effective particle masses emerge from the relaxation dynamics on $\Omega_\chi$, organised into
      a discrete cascade indexed by rank $n$.
      At each rank, the connectivity of the substrate is encoded in a Ramanujan--LPS Cayley graph
      $G(n) = X^{p(n),q}$ of degree $d(n) = p(n)+1$, where $p(n)$ is the effective valence
      parameter~\cite{Beau2026a6}.
      As $p(n)$ grows, the Kesten--McKay spectral support contracts toward $\lambda = 1$, causing
      fixed ADE eigenvalue levels to exit the support at distinct cascade ranks and generating the
      observed mass ordering~\cite{Beau2026a7}.

      The inter-generational mass ratios are governed by
      \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:mass-ratio}
        \frac{M_i}{M_j} \approx
        \left(\frac{|\lambda_i - 1|}{|\lambda_j - 1|}\right)^{2/\beta},
      \end{equation}
      where $\lambda_i$ are fixed ADE eigenvalues and $\beta$ is the exponent in $p(n) \sim n^\beta$~\cite{Beau2026a7}.
      Matching~\eqref{eq:mass-ratio} to the charged lepton hierarchy yields
      $\betastar \in (0.09, 0.13)$, but leaves $\beta$ as a phenomenological input.

    \subsection{What O4 establishes and what remains open}

      The companion paper O4~\cite{Beau2026a8} shows that the bounded-flux constraint
      $|\partial_t \chi_v| \le c_{\mathrm{BI}}$, inherited from Born--Infeld saturation~\cite{Beau2026c},
      limits the advance of the exploration front:
      \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:O4-front}
        \Delta p(n) \lesssim c_{\mathrm{BI}} \sqrt{p(n)},
      \end{equation}
      which integrates to
      \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:O4-quad}
        p(n) \lesssim \tfrac{1}{4} c_{\mathrm{BI}}^2 \, n^2.
      \end{equation}
      Under the convention $p(n) \sim n^\beta$, this implies $\beta \le 2$ algebraically.
      The tighter claim $\beta \le 1$ of~\cite{Beau2026a8} identifies the saturated case as the
      maximal growth rate and restricts attention to $\beta \in (0,1]$.
      In either convention, the factor of $10$--$20$ between this bound and the phenomenological
      window $\betastar \approx 0.1$ is left unexplained.

      The present paper is O5 in the series.
      Its objective is to identify which mechanisms can and cannot bridge this gap.

    \subsection{Main results}

      The central contributions are the following.

      \textbf{Theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation}).}
      The vertex-based admissible span $\Pi_A(S)$ satisfies
      $\dim \Pi_A(S) \le \rank(\Madm) \le |\Cl(G)| = O(q)$
      for all $S \subseteq G = \PSL(2,\F_q)$.
      Beyond a saturation threshold $|\Sstar| = O(q) \ll |G|$,
      the vertex-based admissible frontier $\dA S$ is empty while the raw frontier $\partial S$
      remains $\Omega(\sqrt{|S|})$.

      \textbf{Negative structural results (Section~\ref{subsec:transition-frontier}).}
      Character-based transition fingerprints and fixed-dimensional matrix proxies are
      insufficient to produce a $q$-structural saturation of the admissible frontier.
      The first structurally valid construction is the Steinberg-based fingerprint on
      $\mathbb{P}^1(\F_q)$, with $|\Sstar| \sim q^{1.15}$ and $|\Sstar|/|G| \to 0$.

      \textbf{Localisation result (Remark~\ref{rem:dim-to-dynred}).}
      The smallness of $\betastar$ cannot arise from a representation-theoretic dimension effect.
      It must be a genuinely dynamical redundancy phenomenon in a matrix-level transition space
      of growing dimension.

    \subsection{Organisation}

      Section~\ref{sec:admissible-frontier} introduces the admissible frontier framework and proves

  \section{Admissible Frontier Saturation}
    \label{sec:admissible-frontier}


    The central difficulty identified in~\cite{Beau2026a8} is the following.
    The LPS Cayley graph $X^{p,q}$ is Ramanujan: its combinatorial expansion is optimal, and the
    raw boundary $|\partial S_n|$ grows robustly along any BFS cascade.
    Yet the argument of~\cite{Beau2026a8} establishes only the quadratic upper bound
    \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:O4-bound}
      p(n) \lesssim \tfrac{1}{4} c_{\mathrm{BI}}^2 \, n^2,
    \end{equation}
    derived by integrating $\Delta p(n) \lesssim c_{\mathrm{BI}}\sqrt{p(n)}$ under the closure hypothesis
    $p(n) \propto N(n)$.
    Under the convention $p(n) \sim n^\beta$ of the O-series~\cite{Beau2026a7,Beau2026a8},
    this implies $\beta \le 2$ algebraically.
    The tighter formulation $\beta \le 1$ of~\cite{Beau2026a8} identifies the saturated case as
    the maximal admissible growth rate; we adopt this convention here.
    In either interpretation, the bound leaves the phenomenological window
    $\betastar \in (0.09, 0.13)$ of~\cite{Beau2026a7} unexplained by a factor of $10$--$20$.
    The gap cannot be closed by refining the Cheeger bound alone, since the Ramanujan property
    of $X^{p,q}$ implies that Cheeger constants are already near-optimal for the graph
    combinatorics.

    \begin{remark}[Decoupling from the O4 normalisation]
      \label{rem:O4-decoupling}
      The quadratic bound $p(n) \lesssim C n^2$ is the only statement used from~\cite{Beau2026a8}
      in the present work.
      Any further normalisation of $\beta$ is purely conventional and does not affect the arguments
      below.
    \end{remark}

    The resolution proposed here is that the relevant quantity is not the raw boundary $\partial S_n$
    but the \emph{admissibly productive} boundary: the subset of $\partial S_n$ whose elements
    introduce genuinely new directions in the admissible spectral subspace.
    We show that this quantity saturates rapidly and irrevocably, at a scale
    $|\Sstar| = O(|\Cl(G)|) \ll |G|$, independently of the graph's combinatorial expansion.

    \subsection{Admissible sectors and the character matrix}
      \label{subsec:admissible-sectors}

      Let $G = \PSL(2, \F_q)$ with LPS generating set $\mathcal{S}_p \subset G$ of cardinality
      $d = p + 1$.
      We recall from~\cite{Beau2026a1} that the bounded-flux constraint
      $|\partial_t \chi_v| \le c_{\mathrm{BI}}$ induces a spectral admissibility envelope
      \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:admissibility-envelope}
        A_\rho^{\max} = \frac{c_{\mathrm{BI}}}{\sqrt{\lambda_\rho}},
      \end{equation}
      where $\lambda_\rho = d - \mu_\rho$ is the Laplacian eigenvalue of the irreducible sector
      $\rho \in \widehat{G}$.
      The sector $\rho$ is \emph{admissible} if $0 < \lambda_\rho \le \lambda^*$, where
      $\lambda^* = (\sqrt{d-1}+1)^2$ is the Ramanujan spectral bound~\cite{Beau2026a1}.
      We write $\Gadm \subset \widehat{G}$ for the set of admissible sectors.

      \begin{definition}[Admissible character matrix]
        \label{def:admissible-character-matrix}
        The \emph{admissible character matrix} is
        \[
          \Madm \in \R^{|\Cl(G)| \times |\Gadm|},
          \qquad
          (\Madm)_{[g], \rho} := \chi_\rho(g),
        \]
        where $[g]$ denotes the conjugacy class of $g$ and $\chi_\rho$ is the character of $\rho$.
        Rows are indexed by conjugacy classes; columns by admissible sectors.
      \end{definition}

      \begin{remark}[Dimensions]
        \label{rem:dimensions}
        For $G = \PSL(2,\F_q)$ with $q$ an odd prime:
        $|G| = q(q^2-1)/2 = O(q^3)$;
        $|\Cl(G)| = O(q)$;
        $|\Gadm| \le |\widehat{G}| = O(q)$.
        Hence $\Madm$ is $O(q) \times O(q)$ and $\rank(\Madm) \le |\Cl(G)| = O(q)$,
        while the group itself has order $O(q^3)$.
      \end{remark}

    \subsection{The vertex-based admissible fingerprint}
      \label{subsec:vertex-fingerprint}

      \begin{definition}[Vertex-based admissible fingerprint]
        \label{def:vertex-fingerprint}
        For $g \in G$, the \emph{vertex-based admissible fingerprint} is
        \[
          \piA(g) := \bigl(\kap \, \chi_\rho(g)\bigr)_{\rho \in \Gadm}
          \in \R^{|\Gadm|},
        \]
        where $\kap = \mu_\rho / d$ is the normalised adjacency eigenvalue
        (\emph{spectral coupling},~\cite{Beau2026a2}).
        For $S \subseteq G$ define
        \[
          \PiA(S) := \mathrm{span}_{\R}\{\piA(g) : g \in S\}
          \subseteq \R^{|\Gadm|},
        \]
        and the \emph{vertex-based admissible frontier}
        $\dA S := \{v \in \partial S : \piA(v) \notin \PiA(S)\}$.
      \end{definition}

    \subsection{Rapid saturation of the vertex-based admissible span}
      \label{subsec:saturation}

      \begin{proposition}[Uniform saturation of the vertex-based admissible span]
        \label{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation}
        Let $G = \PSL(2, \F_q)$, $\Gadm$ the admissible sectors, and $\piA$, $\PiA(S)$, $\dA S$
        as in Definition~\ref{def:vertex-fingerprint}.
        Then for any $S \subseteq G$:
        \begin{equation}
          \label{eq:rank-bound}
          \dim \PiA(S) \le \rank(\Madm) \le |\Cl(G)|.
        \end{equation}
        There exists a saturation threshold $\Sstar \subseteq G$ with $|\Sstar| \le \rank(\Madm)$
        such that once $\dim \PiA(S) = \rank(\Madm)$, no further element of $G$ can enlarge $\PiA(S)$.
        Consequently:
        \begin{equation}
          \label{eq:frontier-empty}
          \dA S = \varnothing
          \qquad \text{for all } S \supseteq \Sstar.
        \end{equation}
      \end{proposition}

      \begin{proof}
        For every $g \in G$, the character $\chi_\rho(g)$ is a class function depending only on
        $[g] \in \Cl(G)$.
        Hence $\piA(g)$ is constant on each conjugacy class, and the set of all admissible
        fingerprints is contained in the row space of $\Madm$.
        This gives $\dim \PiA(S) \le \rank(\Madm) \le |\Cl(G)|$ for all $S \subseteq G$.
        Since the row space is finite-dimensional, there exists a finite set $\Sstar$ with
        $|\Sstar| \le \rank(\Madm)$ that already spans the full row space.
        Once $\PiA(S) = \rowspan(\Madm)$, every $v \in G$ satisfies $\piA(v) \in \PiA(S)$,
        so $\dA S = \varnothing$.
      \end{proof}

      \begin{corollary}[Admissible productivity bounded by group structure]
        \label{cor:productivity-bound}
        Along any BFS cascade $(S_n)_{n \ge 0}$ on $X^{p,q}$:
        \begin{equation}
          \label{eq:rn-to-zero}
          r_n := \frac{\dim \PiA(S_n)}{|S_n|}
          = O\!\left(\frac{\rank(\Madm)}{|S_n|}\right)
          \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0.
        \end{equation}
        The span $\PiA(S_n)$ saturates at $|S_{n^*}| \le \rank(\Madm) = O(q)$, after which
        $|\dA S_n| = 0$ while $|\partial S_n|$ remains $\Omega(\sqrt{|S_n|})$ by the Cheeger
        inequality for Ramanujan graphs~\cite{Beau2026a8}.
      \end{corollary}

      \begin{remark}[Numerical verification]
        \label{rem:numerical-A}
        For $G = \PSL(2, \F_{13})$ with $p = 5$, $d = 6$:
        $|G| = 4368$, $|\Gadm| = 12$, $\rank(\Madm) = 5$.
        A BFS cascade on $X^{5,13}$ confirms saturation at $|\Sstar| \approx 35$,
        with $|\dA S_n| = 0$ for all $|S_n| \ge 38$, while $|\partial S_n| \sim 160$--$270$.
        The ratio $r_n$ decays as $O(5/|S_n|)$.
        See Figure~\ref{fig:O5-A-vs-B}.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{remark}[Vertex-based bound is a structural upper bound, not the final growth law]
        \label{rem:A-not-O5}
        The fingerprint $\piA(g)$ identifies group elements through conjugacy classes alone,
        capturing global projective redundancy but not transition-level novelty.
        Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation} is therefore a structural upper bound on
        admissible productivity, not yet the dynamical growth law for $\beta$.
      \end{remark}

    \subsection{The transition-based admissible frontier}
      \label{subsec:transition-frontier}

      Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation} motivates a finer notion of admissible
      novelty sensitive to the \emph{transition} by which a boundary vertex is reached.

      \begin{definition}[Transition-based admissible fingerprint (character version)]
        \label{def:transition-fingerprint}
        For $u \in S_n$ and $v = u \cdot s \notin S_n$ with $s \in \mathcal{S}_p$, define
        \[
          \piB(u \to v) := \bigl(\kap \, \chi_\rho(u) \, \chi_\rho(v)\bigr)_{\rho \in \Gadm}
          \in \R^{|\Gadm|},
        \]
        with $\PiB(S_n) := \mathrm{span}_\R\{\piB(u\to v) :
        u \in S_n,\, v = us \notin S_n,\, s \in \mathcal{S}_p\}$
        and $\dB S_n := \{v \in \partial S_n : \exists u,s,\; \piB(u\to v) \notin \PiB(S_n)\}$.
      \end{definition}

      \begin{remark}[Hierarchy of admissible frontiers]
        \label{rem:frontier-hierarchy}
        By construction $\dA S_n \subseteq \dB S_n \subseteq \partial S_n$.
        Version A is the coarsest (vertices identified by conjugacy class);
        the raw boundary is the finest.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{remark}[Limitation of the character-based fingerprint]
        \label{rem:character-B-limitation}
        Definition~\ref{def:transition-fingerprint} uses only character data.
        On LPS graphs, the generators $\mathcal{S}_p$ lie in a single conjugacy class, so
        $\chi_\rho(s)$ is constant across $s \in \mathcal{S}_p$ for every $\rho$.
        The fingerprint $\piB(u\to v)$ therefore carries no directional information about the
        generator $s$ and collapses to essentially the same saturation behaviour as Version~A.
        A relational refinement using representation matrices $\rho(s)$ is required.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{remark}[Numerical evidence for the character-based limitation]
        \label{rem:numerical-B}
        The character-based Version~B was tested on $X^{5,13}$ and $X^{5,29}$.
        The ratio $\tilde{r}_n^B = |\dB S_n|/|\partial S_n|$ remains near $0.68$ for
        $X^{5,13}$ and near $0.77$ for $X^{5,29}$ after the initial transient, with no
        visible decay toward zero.
        This is consistent with the structural diagnosis of
        Remark~\ref{rem:character-B-limitation}: character collapse on LPS graphs prevents
        the transition fingerprint from resolving genuine directional novelty.
        See Figure~\ref{fig:O5-versionB-saturation}.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{remark}[Matrix-based transition fingerprint and the Steinberg prototype]
        \label{rem:matrix-B}
        A structurally adequate transition fingerprint should use representation operators:
        \[
          \Pi_\rho(u, s) \sim \rho(u)\,\rho(s), \qquad v = us.
        \]
        A prototype was implemented via the permutation action of $G$ on $\mathbb{P}^1(\F_q)$
        ($q+1$ points).
        Each element $g$ induces a permutation $\sigma_g$; the Steinberg projection
        $\tilde{\sigma}_g = \sigma_g - \mathrm{mean}(\sigma_g)$ lies in the $q$-dimensional Steinberg
        component.
        The fingerprint $\pi_B^{\mathrm{St}}(u\to v) = \tilde{\sigma}_u \odot \tilde{\sigma}_s
        \in \R^{q+1}$ is $O(q)$-dimensional and depends on $u$ and $s$ as matrices.
        Crucially, the LPS generators have six distinct Steinberg projections (while lying in
        only two conjugacy classes), breaking the character-level degeneracy.

        Numerical tests on $X^{5,q}$ for $q \in \{13, 17, 29\}$ yield:
        \begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Saturation threshold $|S^*_{\mathrm{St}}| \approx 16, 21, 40$, scaling as
$|S^*_{\mathrm{St}}| \sim q^{1.15}$, \emph{growing with $q$}.
\item Ratio $|S^*_{\mathrm{St}}|/|G| \sim q^{-1.85} \to 0$: structurally meaningful.
\item Cumulative admissible front: $p_n^{\mathrm{prod}} \sim |S_n|^{1.69}$
(stable, $R^2 > 0.997$), outside $\betastar \in (0.09, 0.13)$.
        \end{itemize}
        The exponent $\betaprod \approx 1.69$ governs only the pre-saturation regime of this
        $O(q)$-dimensional proxy; a representation of larger growing dimension is needed to
        reach the physically relevant asymptotic regime.
        See Figures~\ref{fig:O5-matB-variants} and~\ref{fig:O5-StElem-presat}.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{conjecture}[Matrix-level admissible frontier decay]
        \label{conj:edge-admissible-decay}
        There exists a matrix-based definition of the transition-level admissible frontier,
        refining Definition~\ref{def:transition-fingerprint}, such that
        \begin{equation}
          \label{eq:rtilde-mat-decay}
          \rtildeB := \frac{|\partial^{B,\mathrm{mat}} S_n|}{|\partial S_n|}
          \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0,
        \end{equation}
        and the decay of $\rtildeB$ governs the effective cascade exponent via
        \begin{equation}
          \label{eq:beta-from-rtilde-mat}
          \frac{dp}{dn} \lesssim \rtildeB \cdot \sqrt{p(n)}.
        \end{equation}
      \end{conjecture}

      \begin{remark}[Scaling implication]
        \label{rem:beta-scaling-caution}
        Under the power-law Ansatz $\rtildeB \sim p(n)^{-\alpha}$,
        equation~\eqref{eq:beta-from-rtilde-mat} integrates to
        \begin{equation}
          \label{eq:beta-from-alpha}
          \betaeff = \frac{1}{1/2 + \alpha}.
        \end{equation}
        The window $\betastar \in (0.09, 0.13)$ requires $\alpha \approx 9.5$.
        A soft polynomial decay is therefore insufficient; the required suppression must be
        substantially stronger, e.g.\ a matrix-level saturation-of-support mechanism analogous
        to Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation}.
      \end{remark}

    \subsection{Summary of contributions}
      \label{subsec:contributions-summary}

      \textbf{Claim 1 (Structural theorem).}
      Vertex-level admissible novelty saturates at scale $O(q) \ll |G| = O(q^3)$
      (Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation} and
      Corollary~\ref{cor:productivity-bound}).
      This is proved from the representation theory of $\PSL(2,\F_q)$ alone.

      \textbf{Claim 2 (Negative structural result).}
      Character-based transition fingerprints fail on LPS graphs: the generators lie in a single
      conjugacy class, so $\chi_\rho(s)$ is constant and no directional information is captured
      (Remarks~\ref{rem:character-B-limitation}--\ref{rem:numerical-B}).

      \textbf{Claim 3 (Negative proxy result).}
      A matrix fingerprint of fixed ambient dimension ($\dim = O(1)$) produces an extinction of
      $\tilde{r}_n$ but with a $q$-independent threshold, disqualifying it as a structural
      mechanism.
      The Steinberg prototype achieves the first $q$-structural saturation
      ($|S^*| \sim q^{1.15}$, $|S^*|/|G| \to 0$) but at insufficient scale for
      $\betaeff$ measurement.

      \textbf{Consequence.}
      The smallness of $\betastar$ is not a dimension effect at the character level
      (Claim~1 is too rigid), nor at the character-transition level (Claim~2), nor at the
      $q$-independent matrix level (Claim~3).
      The correct mechanism is a dynamical redundancy in a matrix-valued space of
      growing dimension.

    \subsection{Structural implications}
      \label{subsec:structural-implications}

      Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation} is a group-theoretic fact following
      from the admissibility filter, with no appeal to dynamics.
      The dynamical question -- why is $\beta$ small? -- is localised precisely in
      Conjecture~\ref{conj:edge-admissible-decay}: at what rate does the matrix-level
      productive frontier decay along the BFS exploration of $X^{p,q}$?

      \begin{remark}[From dimensional saturation to dynamical redundancy]
        \label{rem:dim-to-dynred}
        Proposition~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation} confines vertex-level novelty to a
        space of dimension $\rank(\Madm) = O(q)$.
        By contrast, a matrix-level fingerprint $\rho(u)\rho(s)$ lives in $\End(V_\rho)$, so the
        full matrix-admissible space has dimension
        \[
          \sum_{\rho \in \Gadm} (\dim \rho)^2 = O(q^3) \sim |G|.
        \]
        No early dimensional saturation is structurally forced for the matrix-based Version~B.
        The suppression of the admissible frontier that would explain $\betastar$ \emph{cannot}
        arise from dimensional constraints at the matrix level; it must originate from a genuinely
        dynamical mechanism of redundancy.
        \emph{The smallness of $\betastar$ is a dynamical fact, not a representation-theoretic one.}
      \end{remark}

    \subsection{Outlook: toward O6}
      \label{subsec:outlook-O6}

      The hierarchy of mechanisms established in this paper is summarised in
      Table~\ref{tab:mechanisms}.

      \begin{table}[ht]
        \centering
        \caption{Hierarchy of admissible frontier mechanisms}
        \label{tab:mechanisms}
        \begin{tabular}{>{\itshape}l l p{7.5cm}}
          \toprule
          Version & Ambient dim & Status \\
          \midrule
          A (vertex, character) & $O(q)$ &
          Theorem~\ref{prop:vertex-admissible-saturation}: saturation proved,
          window $O(q)$ \\
          B (transition, character) & $O(q)$ &

          (Remark~\ref{rem:character-B-limitation}) \\
          B (transition, fixed matrix) & $O(1)$ &

          B (Steinberg, $\mathbb{P}^1$) & $O(q)$ &

          \bottomrule
        \end{tabular}
      \end{table}

      \paragraph{Conclusion and outlook.}
        The admissible frontier analysis isolates the structural origin of the effective hierarchy
        left undetermined by O4.
        At the vertex level, admissible novelty is provably confined to an $O(q)$-dimensional space,
        excluding any purely vertex-based explanation of small effective exponents.
        At the transition level, character-based fingerprints fail on LPS graphs, while
        fixed-dimensional matrix proxies produce an artificial extinction driven by ambient
        dimension rather than group structure.

        The first structurally consistent construction is the Steinberg-based fingerprint,
        whose saturation scale grows as $|S^*| \sim q^{1.15}$ with $|S^*|/|G| \to 0$.
        However, the pre-saturation regime remains too short to extract a reliable
        effective exponent, and the observed $\betaprod \approx 1.7$ lies outside the
        phenomenological window.

        These results collectively demonstrate that the smallness of the cascade exponent
        cannot be explained by low-dimensional admissible quotients, and must instead arise
        from a genuinely dynamical mechanism of redundancy in a matrix-level transition space
        of growing dimension.
        This identifies the minimal structural requirement for a viable theory of admissible
        frontier decay, and motivates the next step: the construction of a transition-level
        fingerprint in a space of dimension $O(q^2)$, where a sufficiently extended
        pre-saturation regime may allow a stable effective exponent to emerge.
